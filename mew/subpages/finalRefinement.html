Final Refinement

Bubble App: Redefining Social Connections for Young Professionals

The Bubble App is a mobile app that fosters building platonic relationships through shared community experiences, catering towards young professionals relocating to new and unfamiliar cities. The app’s intuitive design offers three views for event browsing - a personalized and aesthetic Bubble view, a location-centric Map view, and an information-focused List view, appealing to a diverse set of user needs and preferences.

Figure 1: App Logo

The user’s journey through the app transitions from exploring to attending events by allowing users to bookmark, join group chats, and RVSP to events. User authentication ensures privacy, with limited profile data displayed to other users. The app also aims to support the development of meaningful connections by encouraging users to attend events and engage in conversations through the chat feature.

Refining Brilliance: Crafting the Design Path

During the Figma final design phase, our focus centered on organizing key high-level elements such as the main page, event page, and chats. We wanted to ensure a cohesive and user-friendly interface. Iterative refinement involving user tests allowed us to crystallize our vision while trimming down the list of potential features for a more focused app.

1. Response to Paper Prototyping User Feedback for Heuristic Evaluation

Users responded positively to the use of bubbles for displaying events in our Bubble app. The bubble interface was described as “fun” and appealing to interact with. While this approach resonated well with the majority of users, a subset expressed a desire for alternative views to explore events further. Specifically, some users suggested considering a map view or a calendar view as alternative options. We considered user feedback and integrated a map view into our events page design and a calendar on the navigation bar to view all the events after saving them. Incorporating both the map view and a calendar offered a more valuable way to engage with the events.

2. Responses to Team-Internal Deliberations

In our previous phrase, navigating event details and chats prompted discussion on optimal access – considering a dedicated button or linking through the profile screen. We also explored bubble-inspired designs for group chat access, with a format resembling Instagram’s Highlights (Figure 2). The inclusion of a map feature sparked discussion on necessity and placement, leading to considerations like a map icon in the navigation bar or a toggle between bubble and map views. For aesthetics, a survey among potential users revealed a sleek blue and purple theme as the favored color scheme. Recognizing the importance of user customization, filters were identified as crucial for tailoring events based on specific criteria, such as locations, activity types, and age requirements.

Figure 2: Instagram Story Highlights (left) compared to Bubble chats (right)

3. Responses to A/B Testing

After creating a formal usability plan and testing it with users, there were common themes:

1. Map view: Users appreciate the map view for gauging event proximity but desire clearer location indicators and a legend for color coding. Some find it less intuitive than the list view.

2. List view: Generally favored for its clarity and comprehensive display of information. Users appreciate its organization and straightforward presentation.

3. Bubble View: Seen as aesthetically pleasing but questioned for its practicality, with suggestions to include more event details without compromising aesthetics. Some find it engaging but not as informative as the list view.

4. Icons and Buttons: Some confusion was noted, particularly with the RSVP and bookmark icons, suggesting the need for clearer signifiers or tooltips.

5. Filtering and Searching: Users generally appreciate the functionality but, in some cases, overlook icons on the bubble view.

In summary, the overall takeaway from user feedback on our design was the aesthetic appeal of the Bubble view. The visually engaging nature of this interface element was widely acknowledged, yet users also expressed a collective desire for more detailed event information within the Bubble view to enhance its practical utility. Clarity and intuitive design emerged as the most important considerations, particularly in the realm of icons and buttons, where users highlighted a need for clear signifiers and tooltips to resolve confusions.

Another noteworthy observation was the variance in user preferences regarding the default screen arrangement. While some users favored the Bubble in the middle for its perceived centrality, others leaned towards having it on the left, highlighting the importance of catering to diverse user expectations in interface design.

Furthermore, users consistently placed value on comprehensive information, particularly within the List view. Its organizational structure and ability to present detailed information upfront resonated positively with users. Simultaneously, the Map view earned appreciation for its visual representation of event locations, emphasizing its contribution to the overall user experience. These insights collectively underscored the nuanced balance required between aesthetics, functionality, and user preferences in refining the List-Bubble-Map interface for an optimized and user-centric design.

Event Browsing

The event browsing page marks the start of the main flow of the app, as the first screen that is displayed to the user upon loading into the app (Figure 3). It consists of three different UIs, which can be navigated through with toggle buttons at the top of the screen. Each UI caters to a different set of needs and desires that we found our users expressed.

Figure 3: The Bubble view for event browsing

With the current design, the Bubble view is the default event-browsing view that users will first see upon opening the app (Figure 3). As a landing page of the entire app, it tied the app’s bubble-themed brand into an aesthetic and functional way to focus on events appealing to the user’s personal interests. Events meeting the criteria specified in the filters are displayed with random colors and in a random order within the screen. The size of the bubble conveys important information: a larger bubble size indicates more of an alignment between the event and the user’s predicted interests. As a stylistic feature of this UI, the bubbles slowly and idly scroll toward the top of the screen when left alone.

In the Bubble view, “filter” and “search” buttons were implemented to further customize and streamline a user’s event browsing experience (Figure 4). However, when tested by users, these two buttons frequently fell under the radar. Only once the buttons were explicitly pointed out to them were many of the usability testers able to recognize their value. In response to this, many testers expressed wishes to make the buttons more obvious to a first-time user. This highlighted an important issue with the design of these buttons, especially when comparing them to the effectiveness of similar buttons in the List view (Figure 5). Likely the List view’s layout contributed to this; the search bar, sort-by dropdown, and filter dropdown all take up quite a lot of space in the vertical center of the screen, making them difficult to miss. However, in the Bubble view, the search and filter buttons are quite small and on the edges of the screen. They’re placed on the sides of the toggle button group, which ends up overshadowing the much smaller, less colorful icons.

Figure 4: Search and Filter buttons on the left and right sides of the toggle, respectively

Figure 5: Search and Filter buttons in the List view

To accompany the Bubble view are 2 alternative views: Map and List, which are intended to be secondary to the Bubble view. The Map view contains a scrollable map with small bubble markers to indicate where events are occurring, but doesn’t initially contain any visible text (Figure 6). Therefore, the Map view appeals to users who prioritize event location over actual event details, perhaps if traveling was a limiting factor as one A/B tester mentioned. Two other testers also expressed that they liked seeing things happening around them, which made this view appealing. Clicking on a map marker displays a hover card to preview event information, and the user can then navigate to the actual Event Details screen if the event appeals to them (Figure 6).

Figure 6: Map View

Originally, the design consisted only of the Bubble and Map views (Figure 7). After conversations stemming from a heuristic evaluation of the prototype, we recognized a desire for a third alternative view that listed events and details in a more standard, text-based format. The general sentiment was that the Bubble view was a visually pleasing and dynamic version of a list, but it was difficult to quickly scan event names due to the irregularity of the text placement. Additionally, users wanted to be able to efficiently see a lot of the event’s information (date, time, location, etc), which wasn’t suitable for the sleek look of the Bubble view or the text-free design of the Map view, but was well-suited to a standard list format.

Figure 7: Original prototype with only Bubble and Map views

Overall, the List view fulfills user desires to see a “less graphical” layout of events (Figure 8). The design balances displaying plentiful information in an easily-parsable format without overwhelming users with the amount of text on the screen. Additionally, the search bar and filter options are more obvious on this view than other views because this List view relies entirely on user input while browsing, instead of algorithm recommendations or location.

Figure 8: List View

Event Browsing: Toggle Buttons

Within the toggle button group, the Bubble view button is centered to enforce its hierarchical position, while emphasizing that the other two views are equally valid and useful alternatives. The order of the toggle buttons was determined through discussions with usability test participants, and a survey sent out to a large population of a similar demographic as our target audience of young professionals (18 - 24 year-olds) (Figure 9).

Figure 9: Toggle button order survey

Out of the 4 choices, 34% of survey respondents indicated a preference for the order “List - Bubble - Toggle”. Having a central Bubble view button was significantly more popular; some participants referenced Snapchat as a familiar UI, with the default/main view as the center button. From there, the ideal order of the List and Map buttons was not so clear-cut based on how close the survey results are. However, some of our design usability testers offered clear reasons for supporting the List-Bubble-Map order: having the List view as the leftmost option felt intuitive due to the inherent left alignment of the displayed text in the UI.

Event Details

The event details page is what users see when they click on an event in any event browsing view. From this page, users can read more information about the event and choose to RSVP using the save button. This page is fairly one-to-one with our original heuristic testing prototype, as users found the layout conducive and the information provided sensible (Figure 10). During A/B testing, as well, users specifically pointed out that they enjoyed seeing the “Who’s Going” list and profile pics of friends around the event image.

Figure 10: Current design (left) compared to original prototype (right)

The one point of confusion that users ran into was the RSVP button itself. We used the bookmark icon, and depending on the specific word used to ask a user to perform the task, they had difficulty realizing the bookmark was the button they needed to press. When told to “save” an event, users easily identified the bookmark as the correct option. When told to “join” an event, the button’s purpose was less obvious. The buttons to add an event to calendar and add yourself to an event chat both had icons that were clear, but the bookmark with the intention of “mark interest in an event” was nebulous.

Perhaps if we had developed a profile page where we intended users could view the events they bookmarked, the function would be more clear. However, for these runs we focused more heavily on the event browsing features instead of expanding the technicalities of the joining-event options.

Our future studies might include another round of testing with that profile page to gauge user understanding. Alternatively, we might just change the bookmark button to a more clarified “Join” or “Save” text button, forgoing the aesthetic of icon-only buttons in favor of increased user comparability.

Heuristic Evaluation Response:

Our violations of severity 3 or higher can be grouped into five different categories: User controls, Error handling and prevention, Instruction integration, Help and support, and Bug fixes and future features

User Controls:

Two violations addressed user controls:

1. Will there be a way to leave a chat if you no longer want to be involved or joined by accident?

2. Will there be the typical chat features of muting notifications, leaving, @-ing someone? Most of these I assume aren’t implemented because it’s a prototype but I think this would be important to have so a user has control over the chat group feature.

Like most chatting interfaces, the Bubble App’s design ensures that users have full control over their participation. They will be able to leave chats, mute notifications, and @ other users in a chat. Users will also be able to un-RSVP and unsave events at any point after joining the associated chat; nothing about joining a chat is binding to users. Additionally, to make all users feel comfortable with using our app, they will be able to block and report other users who make them uncomfortable.

Error Handling and Prevention:

Two violations addressed error handling and prevention:

1. None was included in the current prototype, what happens when people try to make an event without a name? Or without participants? How would you handle users who are trying to do things with the app that it is not supposed to?

2. I don’t think there was any error prevention in the prototype but it should definitely be implemented. Having checks for giving complete event info, leaving chats if added mistakenly, etc should definitely be included.

To maintain the quality of interactions, the app will not allow events to be created without essential information like event name, location, and time/date. There will be input validation implemented to prevent submitting invalid data. Additionally, users can report inappropriate content or behavior, ensuring the community remains aligned with the app’s spirit. We’ve addressed the error prevention concerns by implementing confirmation dialogs before submitting every destructive or important interaction, such as when users RSVP to events, leave chats, delete an event, etc.

Instruction Integration:

One violation addressed instruction integration:

1. How could you integrate your instructions in such a way that it wouldn't be intrusive to a user experience? What other documentation could you need? I imagine having instructions for event creation must be important

We now offer an onboarding tutorial that guides new users through the app’s features. The tutorial is non-intrusive and easy to follow along to. Additionally, in the future, there will be a button that allows users to replay this tutorial at any time.

Help and Support:

One violation addressed help and support:

1. I didn’t see a help button but… I don’t think it’s needed at this phase of prototyping, especially considering other apps I’ve used will tend to keep this information in the settings tab and I don’t think any team is focusing on making a settings tab right now

Currently, we don’t have help buttons integrated in the app. Our future design plans involve screens consisting of a lot of data (such as the Event Browsing pages) having a small question mark icon in the top right corner. Upon clicking this icon, a dialog will appear containing information about the current screen’s purpose and behavior. This design ensures users will always have a way to refresh their intuition about a specific page without it obnoxiously detracting from the overall experience.

Bug Fixes and Future Features:

Two violations addressed bug fixes and future features:

1. If I click on the chat button and cancel to go back to the event page and then click the chat again it automatically adds me.

2. Mentions mutual friends? Is there a friending feature? If so, I’m unclear on how to get there.

We’ve fixed the issue where clicking on the chat button automatically added users without confirmation; the current design displays a confirmation dialog before the user is added. Additionally, there will be a friending feature similar to platforms like Facebook, allowing users to connect and see when mutual friends attend the same events as them. Although this feature wasn’t part of the prototype for the heuristic evaluation, it’s an essential addition to the app’s functionality and will be implemented in the full design.


Overall, the level 3+ violations illuminated many important features and behaviors that were lacking in the app’s original design. Our adjustments and additions in response to the violations aim to enhance user experience, provide necessary guidance, and maintain a secure and enjoyable environment within the app

Future Design Recommendations

Based on user feedback from our A/B testing, a notable comment highlighted the dominance of the color blue on our platform, prompting us to address and enhance color contrast for better accessibility and readability. To achieve this, we can prioritize color combinations with a substantial contrast ratio, ensuring that text and crucial elements visibly stand out against the background. Additionally, considering the contrast between text and its background, such as favoring dark text on a light background or vice versa, can contribute to improved visibility; being mindful of red-green combinations is crucial to accommodate users with color blindness.

Thorough testing of color combinations across different devices is essential, recognizing that colors may vary across screens. Adhering to accessibility standards, particularly those outlined in WCAG, and aiming for recommended contrast ratios will further enhance the user experience. Actively incorporating user feedback from A/B testing allows us to gain insights into specific concerns and suggestions. Providing options for users to customize color schemes and adjust text sizes can address diverse user needs. Leveraging color accessibility tools and conducting regular tests for improvements are essential steps toward fostering a positive and inclusive user experience on our platform.

To enhance accessibility, we tested our color scheme with settings for color insensitivity and reduced blue sensitivity (Figure 11). Finding sufficient contrast, we would like to do user testing with individuals with visual disabilities for future design.
